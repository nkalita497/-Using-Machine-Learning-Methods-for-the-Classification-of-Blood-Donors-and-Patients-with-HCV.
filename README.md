This study explores the feasibility of using machine learning to automatically assess liver disease stages based on routine biochemical tests. The primary objective was to distinguish healthy blood donors from HCV-infected patients, a significant challenge given the severe class imbalance in the available dataset.
Among the six tested algorithms, Gradient Boosting achieved the best performance. On the test set, the model reached an F1 score of 0.873 and an AUC-ROC of 0.989. However, cross-validation provided a more realistic estimation of the task's difficulty (F1 = 0.626), highlighting the impact of the imbalanced data. Importantly, the algorithm's decision-making process proved fully consistent with current hepatological knowledge. SHAP analysis confirmed that the model relied on key clinical predictors: patient age and sex, albumin levels, and the activity of ALT and ALP enzymes.
Despite its high accuracy, from a clinical perspective, the proposed solution is better suited as a decision-support tool for initial patient triage rather than a standalone diagnostic instrument. A detailed error analysis revealed that the model struggles most with identifying hepatitis, occasionally misclassifying infected individuals as healthy blood donors - a critical error in a real-world setting. Furthermore, the model tends to confuse adjacent disease stages, which accurately reflects the real diagnostic challenges faced by clinicians relying solely on blood parameters.
The experiment confirms the potential of machine learning in analyzing laboratory data while also outlining specific requirements for future systems. To ensure such tools are safe and fully operational, acquiring a larger dataset for the minority classes is essential. Future development of this approach could involve integrating blood test data with medical history and imaging results, or reframing the model architecture as a hierarchical classification problem.
